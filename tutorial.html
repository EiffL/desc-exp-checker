Tutorial

The Viewer automatically pulls an image, which you should inspect and determine if it's fine. If so, simply press "Next". If not, select one of the predefined artifacts from the "Hold on, that's..." button. Once you're done marking the problems, click "Next".

Cosmic rays

They are an inevitable contaminant to images from our deep red-sensitive CCDs, so the problem is not that they are there. It's when they are not properly masked. The good thing about them: they are easy to spot. Use the display options to select of fairly bright setting, e.g. , and they will be very apparent. Toggle the mask to find out if all have been found and whether they are entirely masked.

It's perfectly fine to mark multiple cosmic rays per image, or multiple different problems on the same image. If you want to redo your selection, simply clear the markings from the image. If you're satisfied, save it and move on the the next image.

Ghosts

Ghost are reflections of the optics, basically an undesired additional path from the outside of the telescope to CCD. They occur all the time, but only show up if a bright object provides enough illumination for this extra optical path. Then, often very large areas (from parts of a chip to the entire focal plane) show ring-like features, which are brighter or fainter than the surrounding sky. Whether ghosts are of concern depends strongly on the kind of science analysis you're after. In either case, we need to know where they are.

Factor 2 zoom-out of a pupil ghost next to a bright star.

Cross-talk

Cross-talk is a readout issue, which occurs in the electronics. The visual imprint of cross-talk is a long, horizontal excess of counts, typically several pixels wide and often widest in the middle section. When the cross-talk occurs on a single chip, there should be a bright object on the other side of the horizontal A/B transition.

Since cross-talk scales with the brightness of the object that cause it, only the brightest objects (typically stars) will leave a clearly visible imprint. Switch to scaling, and potentially the visible range, to dig deeper into noise. Since the feature can be very extended, mark the region where it has it's largest vertical extension: the knot in the middle.

Cross-talk from the bright star in the bottom onto the top amplifier.

Satellite

Satellite or planes are expected to cross our focal plane several times per hour. They create long streaks on the images, much longer and wider than cosmic rays. Since they are so large, you can mark the track whereever you want. Sometimes, however, the tracks are broken into segments (remember: planes have blinking lights), in which case we'd like to have marks on each of the segments.

Satellite or plane track

Readout

There are additional readout glitches, which can simply be found by looking for horizontal (= readout direction) features, almost always exactly one pixel wide. Mark it where it is most evident on the image.

Unmasked readout glitch (top). In addition, there is also a cosmic ray hit and a fainter cross-talk.

Failures

Sometimes a large portion of the entire image is affected by, well, something. It would be too bothersome to mark each single feature on such an image, and even if, it would probably be hard to train a piece of software to distinguish between the contaminated parts and the usable rest. So, we'll settle for finding them first.

Before marking an image as catastrophic failure, please make sure that it is not just a conincidence of several other features. As a guideline, estimate the remaining usable area after everything problematic has been masked (which may or may not be the case in the image you're are looking at). If this remainder is less than 50%, mark the image as catastrophic.

Other...

Sometimes, none of the other categories fit. In this case, you can describe the problem in your own words. Please do so in a way that is comprehensible inasmuch as possible. Later on, we will attempt to categorize the problems you have identified and incorporate reocurring issues for later versions of this application.

Awesome!

Rarely, you'll come across a truely phenomenal sight, maybe of a bunch of interacting galaxies or of a comet, a star cluster, etc. Please do report these objects by selecting "Awesome!" from the drop-down, then describe what you see and click on the object. We collect these objects, and present the most fabulous ones in our Gallery.

If you'd like to help with the inspection of the candidates for our website and outreach purposes, please contact us.

Can I help?

Certainly. If you think you found a new class of artifacts, let us know. If you know where these artefacts come from, let us know. How? Go to the redmine wiki, where we list and discuss such cases.
